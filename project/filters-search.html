Filters & Search

Goal

To increase the efficiency of workflow of photos in the Inbox and the Approved, two main parts of our enterprise platform.

  • Inbox: where collected social media photos are trashed or kept (moved to Approved)
  • Approved: where the kept photos are managed: tagging products, requesting permission, organizing into other albums
The two sections of our albums: the inbox and the approved

Gathering User Feedback

My PM and I first sat down with the customer success team to hear the general trends of our customers' complaints regarding workflow issues. The PM then went through the customer logs as I watched screen recordings of customers that were heavy users of this part of our platform. Of these users, I jumped on the phone with 5 that spanned the gamut of our customer base to gather more information regarding their needs and workflows.

Synthesis

The notes above were further synthesized and distilled into 3 general trends:

Research distilled into 3 key findings

Very quickly, we realized that there were some quick wins and some that would take much more engineering:

  • Easier: implement more robust searches and filters to help users find the relevant photos (ie: photos that don't have products tagged yet, photos where the submitters' permissions haven't been requested).
  • Harder: our current computer vision and spam detection were not serving the needs of all our varied customers. Currently, the same types of computer vision and spam detection were being applied to all customers, regardless of industry (ski resort vs bathing suit company) or company size (Sleefs vs Carnival Cruises) .

Our research helped us confirm the issues within the Inbox and Approved parts of our albums and helped us navigate which features to build, but we split the work up across several projects. We first worked on the easiest win: adding more search and filter capabilities - this is what the remainder of this case review will cover. Since then we have also implemented better computer vision and spam detection, but because there was minimal design work from my end, I will leave that part out of this project review.

Search

Our old search was small and marginalized, making it difficult to find

Search Issues

From all the research, the key issues with our search, regardless of being in Inbox or Approved, were...

  • Search was difficult to find
  • It was not obvious what was searchable
  • When searching usernames and hashtags, are @ and # required?
  • Needed more search capabilities:
    • In addition to usernames, hashtags, and captions, users also needed a way to search for products tagged in the photos, find text written within internal notes of the photo, and figure out what other albums that the photos belonged to
    • Users also wanted to search for opposites, for example, find photos that are not tagged #nike.

Engineering Constraints

During a conversation with our backend engineer, he mentioned that especially with the additional search functionalities, a blanketed search (typing “shoe” without designating if this is hashtag, caption, username, product, etc) is highly costly when searching through millions of photos within our database.

Our design solution needed a way for the user to designate what type of search they're performing. For example, I'm searching Nike, a product name, not the hashtag, userhandle, or a word in the caption.

Design Research & Sketches

Our search needed to be very robust, but search itself isn't a new problem, so the first thing I did was look through many consumer and enterprise platforms' search/filter capabilities to see how they were structured.

Design research into robust searches in both enterprise and consumer platforms

With a good idea of how I'd like to structure our search, I drew some quick sketches to visualize what our search would look like, which I then took over to Sketch to refine.

Sketches and prototypes

I had prototyped the gist of the interactions in Invision so that the engineer could see my overall vision, but because the interactions were quite nuanced, I worked very closely with the engineer to iron out the kinks. The following are the final design decisions along with the reasoning:

Zoomed in view of new search interactions

Dropdowns

  • display and educate selection choices
  • lends to countless search permutations as users can combine attributes, filters, and text
  • allows for the user to determine if the word “shoe” that they’re searching for is in the caption, hashtag, etc - reduces backend computation

Progressive Disclosure

  • action is taken before subsequent action appears - helps reduce cognitive load and confusion of having 2 dropdowns and input field shown simultaneously

Search Across the Top

  • took the marginalized and difficult-to-find search from the left panel and placed it front and center, allowing the search to take more visual space so that it'll be less easily missed.
  • allowed more space to display all searchable attributes, especially since users needed to be informed by many new search functionalities that we added
Zoomed in view of deleting interaction

Deletion Behavior

  • pressing backspace will delete the text and also the search filters and attributes - allows users to work quickly and in the typing manner that they’re used to

I explored many different ways to present filters, from combining search and filters together, to saving common queries, to hiding/expanding filters along the left, but I ultimately kept the filters exactly where they were originally located.

Sketches of filters

Inbox vs Approved Filters

As mentioned previously, for the most part, Inbox and Approved have different workflows but the workflows varied from user to user. To streamline workflows without disrupting others, our solution was to include relatively the same filters in both Inbox and Approved but organized in terms of what is commonly used inthe Inbox and Approved. This provided efficiency for most without preventing the few from continuing with their current workflows.

Inbox (left) & Approved (right) filters

Reducing Clutter

The first mock had all 40 filters shown and was too overwhelming, so future iterations explored different ways of hiding the filters.

How to best present the filters?

The final design left the important, most-used filters open by default for easy, no-hassle selection. The less important filters were closed to reduce clutter but the user could still easily see the types of filters that are available.

Results & Future Iterations

Within the last 2 months (Sept 2016) since it’s launch,

Average number of searches per month rose by ~50%.

Some other notable findings about search:

  • Different types of searches were conducted in different parts of the platform:
    • Before a photo has been collected, brands are more interested in finding photos by hashtags.
    • But once a photo has been collected, brands are more interested in finding photos with specific products or that have been organized into other albums
  • Regardless of before or after a photo has been collected, a brand will always want to find photos by usernames most.

Unfortunately, I couldn't have access to the data that measured the success of the filters. But, anecdotally, none of our customers have complained about any missing filters.

Future Iterations:

  • Order the drop downs according to the most used searches within that part of the platform
  • Surface the top filters for each individual user and presenting these filters at the top while hiding the rest
  • Also, as our users become more accustomed to this new search, we'll see if requests flow in for "and" and "or" searches. We already have these backend capabilities, but we're waiting for customer requests and validation before building a more robust experience.

As mentioned previously, our research also launched a whole series updates to our computer vision and image recognition. Both are to improve the workflows within Inbox, especially for finding relevant photos to approve and identifying spam to delete.
